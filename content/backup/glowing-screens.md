Lately, I feel like I’m disappearing into a screen. My life is split in two—one inside the glow of my phone, the other in the real world. And somehow, the first one is winning.  

The moment I wake up, before I even stretch or feel the cold floor under my feet, my hand reaches for my phone. Not because I need to. Not because someone is waiting for me. Just… because. It’s a habit, a reflex. Like my day can’t start without that cold blue light hitting my face. My eyes are barely open, and I’m already scrolling through a world that isn’t even real.  

I tell myself I’ll just check one thing. But then minutes turn into half an hour. Half an hour turns into more. Suddenly, I’m running late, rushing through my morning, my mind already cluttered with information I didn’t need. Someone’s vacation photos. A stranger’s opinion. News that makes me feel helpless. A hundred little distractions before I’ve even taken a deep breath.  

And it doesn’t stop there. Throughout the day, my phone is always within reach. When I eat, when I walk, when I should be resting. It’s there, filling every gap, every quiet moment. I catch myself unlocking it without thinking, my fingers moving on their own. Sometimes I don’t even know what I’m looking for. Maybe nothing. Maybe everything.  

I don’t know when it happened, but somewhere along the way, screens became my world. My thoughts, my memories, my conversations—they live inside these glowing rectangles. I write notes I never read. I save songs I never truly listen to. I talk to people without really being with them. And when I step away from the screen, the real world feels strange, too quiet, too uncertain. Talking without typing feels awkward. Silence without scrolling feels heavy.  

The worst part? I let this happen.  

It’s not just about being addicted to my phone. It’s about escaping. The online world is easier. I can control it—what I see, who I talk to, what I avoid. The real world is different. It’s messy. It’s full of moments I can’t edit, people I can’t mute, feelings I can’t filter. Screens don’t judge me. People do.  

But for all its comfort, the digital world is empty. A message can’t replace the feeling of someone’s hand in mine. A video call can’t replace the warmth of someone sitting close, breathing the same air, sharing the same silence.  

The other day, I was with friends. We were laughing—real, deep, from-the-stomach laughter. No phones. No scrolling. Just us. And in that moment, I felt something I hadn’t in a long time—light. Free. Present. It reminded me of being a kid, when fun wasn’t something you captured, just something you felt.  

I miss that. I miss living without thinking about documenting it. I miss experiencing things just for myself, not for a post, not for likes, not for anyone else’s approval.  

Maybe balance isn’t about quitting screens. Maybe it’s about making sure they don’t steal more than they give. It’s about living moments that don’t need to be posted to feel real. It’s about remembering that life is happening right now, outside the glow of a screen, in the messy, imperfect, beautiful world that I don’t want to lose.  

I don’t have all the answers. But I know this—I don’t want to wake up one day and realize that the best years of my life were spent staring at a screen instead of living them.
